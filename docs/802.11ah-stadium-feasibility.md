# 802.11ah (Wi-Fi HaLow) Stadium Feasibility Analysis

## Short Answer

**No, 802.11ah cannot replace the primary infrastructure. It has a narrow but legitimate role as a complementary IoT layer — but even that role is hard to justify given the existing VLAN 60 IoT design already handles it over standard WiFi.**

---

## 1. Can 802.11ah Replace Primary WiFi (802.11ax/be) for Fan Connectivity?

**Absolutely not.** This isn't even close.

| Metric | 802.11ax (Current Design) | 802.11ah (HaLow) |
|--------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Per-client throughput target | 2–5 Mbps | 150 Kbps–4 Mbps (1-2 MHz channels) |
| Realistic per-client in density | 5–15 Mbps | <1 Mbps shared |
| Available spectrum (US) | ~500 MHz (5 GHz) + 1200 MHz (6 GHz) | 26 MHz (902–928 MHz) |
| Non-overlapping 20 MHz channels | 24 (5 GHz) + 59 (6 GHz) | 0 — max channel width is 16 MHz, and there's only 1 of those |
| Smartphone/tablet support | Universal | **Zero** |

The math is fatal: 26 MHz of total spectrum shared across 30,000–80,000 devices. Even at maximum theoretical efficiency with 16 MHz channels (347 Mbps PHY, ~40 Mbps real throughput), you'd get roughly **0.001 Mbps per device** if 40,000 are active. That's not WiFi — that's a pager network.

**Verdict: Impossible. Not viable at any scale.**

---

## 2. Where Could 802.11ah Fit as Complementary Technology?

The only defensible use cases in this stadium context:

- **Outdoor perimeter sensors** — parking lot occupancy, environmental monitoring, perimeter security cameras at distances >150m from the nearest IDF where running Cat6a is impractical
- **Sub-grade/tunnel infrastructure monitoring** — structural health sensors in concrete where 900 MHz penetration advantage matters and there's no existing cable pathway
- **Temporary/portable sensor deployments** — battery-powered devices for pop-up events in parking lots or tailgate zones

But here's the problem: **the existing design already has VLAN 60 (IoT & Sensors) running on the same 802.11ax APs.** Any sensor within 100m of an AP (which is essentially the entire venue interior) is already covered. HaLow only adds value where the existing 2.4 GHz IoT layer can't reach.

---

## 3. Use Cases: Where HaLow Excels vs Fails in a Stadium

**Excels:**
- Battery-powered sensors transmitting small payloads (<1 KB) every 30–300 seconds at extended range (parking structures, perimeter fencing, remote gates)
- Asset tracking tags on equipment across the full campus with minimal AP count

**Fails:**
- Fan connectivity (no device support, insufficient throughput)
- Mobile ticketing / QR scanning (latency too high, no phone support)
- Video streaming, social media uploads (throughput inadequate)
- POS/concession transactions (no HaLow in POS terminals)
- Media/press operations (not even in the conversation)
- Real-time crowd analytics via connected devices (fans don't have HaLow radios)

---

## 4. Throughput/Capacity Analysis at Stadium Scale

Fans need: social media (1–3 Mbps), video streaming (3–8 Mbps), mobile ticketing (0.5 Mbps burst), messaging (0.1 Mbps). Call it **2 Mbps average per active user.**

With 40,000 concurrently active devices × 2 Mbps = **80 Gbps aggregate demand.**

HaLow's entire spectrum (26 MHz, all channels simultaneously, 100% efficiency, zero overhead): **~78 Mbps theoretical maximum.** That's 0.1% of the required capacity. Three orders of magnitude short.

---

## 5. Channel Planning at 900 MHz in a Dense Venue

The 902–928 MHz ISM band in the US gives you:
- 26 × 1 MHz channels (usable throughput: ~150 Kbps each)
- 13 × 2 MHz channels
- 6 × 4 MHz channels
- 3 × 8 MHz channels
- 1 × 16 MHz channel

In a stadium environment you'd face:
- **LoRa interference** — many venues already deploy LoRaWAN for building automation; same band
- **Co-channel reuse is brutal** — with only 3–6 usable wider channels, a 70,000-seat venue would have massive CCI
- **900 MHz propagates far** — great for range, terrible for cell isolation. You *want* small cells in high-density; HaLow's physics work against you
- **No DFS, no AFC** — what you see is all you get

---

## 6. Device Ecosystem Reality Check

**This is the deal-killer.** As of early 2026:

- **Zero mainstream smartphones** support 802.11ah (no iPhone, no Samsung Galaxy, no Pixel)
- **Zero mainstream laptops/tablets** support it
- HaLow chipsets (Morse Micro MM6108, Newracom NRC7394) exist only in purpose-built IoT modules, industrial gateways, and dev kits
- Qualcomm, Broadcom, MediaTek — the companies making WiFi chips for phones — have shown zero interest in adding sub-1 GHz radio chains to mobile SoCs
- Adding a 900 MHz antenna to a phone form factor is physically challenging (wavelength ~33cm; antenna would need to be ~8cm minimum)

**You cannot serve fans with a technology their devices don't support.** This alone ends the conversation for primary connectivity.

---

## 7. Integration with Catalyst 9800 / VLAN Architecture

The current design is built around:
- **Cisco Catalyst 9800 WLC cluster** managing 500–3,000+ APs via CAPWAP
- Centralized switching with 7 VLANs for segmentation
- Cisco C9100-series APs (or similar)

Integration problems with HaLow:
- **Cisco has no HaLow APs.** Neither does Aruba, Ruckus, or any enterprise vendor.
- **Catalyst 9800 doesn't speak HaLow.** No CAPWAP support, no RF profiles, no RRM.
- HaLow APs (Morse Micro-based) are standalone or use proprietary cloud management — a **completely separate management plane**
- You'd need a parallel infrastructure: separate APs, separate controller/cloud, separate monitoring, separate troubleshooting tools
- VLAN integration would require trunking HaLow APs into the access switches independently, with no WLC-based policy enforcement
- This doubles operational complexity for a marginal use case

---

## 8. Cost and Complexity Comparison

| Factor | Existing 802.11ax Design | Adding HaLow Overlay |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| AP cost | Already budgeted (500–3,000 APs) | Additional $200–400/AP × N HaLow APs |
| Controller | Catalyst 9800 (already sized) | Separate management platform (cloud or on-prem) |
| Cabling | Already run for existing APs | New cable runs or PoE injectors for HaLow AP locations |
| Staff training | Cisco-certified team | Second vendor, second toolset, second skill set |
| Monitoring | Cisco DNA Center / Prime | Separate NMS for HaLow |
| Troubleshooting | Unified WLC logs, client tracking | Two parallel systems to correlate |
| Clients served | 100% of fan devices | 0% of fan devices; only purpose-built IoT sensors |

For the IoT sensor use case, you're adding an entire parallel network infrastructure to serve devices that could mostly connect to existing 2.4 GHz on VLAN 60. The cost-benefit is terrible unless you have a specific hard requirement for sensors at >150m from any existing AP with no wired path.

---

## 9. Recommendation

**Do not deploy 802.11ah in this stadium. It adds cost and complexity with negligible benefit.**

The rationale is straightforward:

1. **It cannot serve fans** — zero device support, three orders of magnitude short on capacity
2. **The existing design already handles IoT** — VLAN 60 on 802.11ax/2.4 GHz covers every sensor within the venue footprint
3. **No enterprise vendor support** — creates a management silo outside the Catalyst 9800 ecosystem
4. **The only edge case** (far-perimeter sensors) is better solved with LoRaWAN for sub-1 Mbps telemetry or a few additional 802.11ax APs with external antennas for anything requiring more throughput

If this project had a campus-wide smart agriculture overlay or a 2km outdoor sensor mesh requirement, HaLow would merit discussion. For a stadium serving fans, it's the wrong tool for the job. Invest the money in more 6 GHz-capable APs or upgrading backhaul — that's where the ROI lives.
